## The very beginning
A "computer" actually used to be a job title. It was used for people who would do a crap ton of maths for science, engineering, government, any work in any job that required any maths. But now, we call it the thing you're using the view this article on!

Now, what actually **IS** a computer nowadays? 

It's defined as a **programmable** machine that receives **input**, processes information according to **instructions**, **stores data**, and produces **output**. 

In layman's terms, it's a machine where people can give it things (input), and it'll give them something else (output) depending on what they told it to do (instructions), it has to be able to store things (data) and also it has to work for everyone's personal instructions, inputs, and data. 

So, think about this for a second. If **YOU** were tasked to create a machine that does that exact thing, how would you do so?

## The Transistor
Obviously that's a ginormous task. In fact, you probably need an entire team of dedicated engineers and a LOT of money and time to do so. So let's break down the task into a million different smaller problems that we can solve one by one. 

### A Problem
Well, first let's find a reason we even need something like this to begin with. Let's start with counting. Let's say your friend borrows money from you. He borrows a hundred bucks. But the next time you meet him, he says he doesn't remember he borrowed money! He doesn't even remember the amount! What a frustrating friend.

So you two decide to make a system. We could write it down, but then we have to erase it, write down new numbers, etc... It's just kinda inefficient, and if you have multiple friends borrowing multiple amounts, it gets confusing.

Let's invent a better system. How could we use electricity to count? Let's dig even deeper. 

How do you even represent 1 in real life? Numbers, mathematics, all of these things are abstract concepts. We can use our fingers, we can write it down, but if we use any of those things, we need a human, and then we'll just go back to humans needing to do the maths...

So then the problem updates. How do we represent 1, where the absolute most minimum human effort is required?

### Electricity
We could use electricity! You don't have to know the details, but we at least know that when you flick a light switch, it turns the light on, and if you flick it the other way, the light turns off. What if we represent the number 1 as the light switch being on, and we represent 0 as the light switch being turned off? Now, if your friend borrows 1 dollar, we can turn a light on, which is as simple as flicking a switch. 

But what if your friend borrows more money? Obviously he's not just gonna borrow 1 dollar from you. With inflation that dollar is worth nothing! Well, we can't just measure how bright the light gets to increase the numbers, and light bulbs are kinda expensive, so you don't want to buy 100 lightbulbs to count 100 dollars.
